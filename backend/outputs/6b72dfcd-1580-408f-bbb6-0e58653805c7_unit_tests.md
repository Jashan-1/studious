# Unit Test Questions

## Question 1
When Kisa Gotami first approaches her neighbors after her son's death, what does she ask for, and what is their response?

**Model Answer:**
Kisa Gotami initially asks her neighbors for medicine to cure her deceased son, believing that he can be brought back to life. The neighbors, however, tell her that her son is dead and that she has lost her senses, indicating their disbelief in her request.

**Key Points:**
- Kisa Gotami seeks medicine to cure her dead son.
- The neighbors respond with pity and disbelief.
- This reflects her denial of the reality of her son's death.

## Question 2
After her conversation with the Buddha, what does Kisa Gotami seek from the houses she visits a second time, and what does she learn?

**Model Answer:**
Kisa Gotami seeks a handful of mustard seeds from houses where no one has experienced death. However, she learns that every household has suffered a loss, demonstrating to her that death is a universal experience shared by all.

**Key Points:**
- She asks for mustard seeds from houses without death.
- She discovers that every house has experienced loss.
- This realization highlights the universality of grief and death.

## Question 3
What crucial understanding does Kisa Gotami gain after her second round of visits that she did not grasp initially?

**Model Answer:**
Kisa Gotami realizes that death is a common experience for all people, not just her. Initially, she felt isolated in her grief, but her journey leads her to understand that everyone faces loss, which is a fundamental aspect of life.

**Key Points:**
- Initially, Kisa Gotami feels her grief is unique.
- She learns that death is common to all individuals.
- This understanding aligns with the Buddha's teachings about the nature of mortality.

## Question 4
Why do you think Kisa Gotami's understanding of her grief changes after her interactions with the Buddha and her subsequent experiences?

**Model Answer:**
Kisa Gotami's understanding changes because the Buddha's teachings and her experiences compel her to confront the reality of death. By seeking mustard seeds and discovering the shared nature of grief, she shifts from a focus on her individual sorrow to a broader understanding of human suffering, which the Buddha aimed to impart.

**Key Points:**
- The Buddha's teachings encourage deeper reflection on grief.
- Her search for mustard seeds reveals the universality of loss.
- This shift in perspective fosters acceptance of mortality.
