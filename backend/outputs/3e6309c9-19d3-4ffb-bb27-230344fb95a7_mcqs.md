# Multiple Choice Questions

## Question 1
What was the main lesson Gautama Buddha wanted Kisa Gotami to learn through her quest for mustard seeds?

A. That medicine can only be found at special houses  
B. That death is a universal experience and not unique to her  
C. That she should become a monk  
D. That grief should be hidden from others  

**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The Buddha wanted Kisa Gotami to realize that death comes to all, and her grief was not unique; this understanding would help her overcome her sorrow.

## Question 2
Why did Kisa Gotami fail to obtain the mustard seeds as the Buddha had instructed?

A. No one had mustard seeds  
B. She was too tired to search  
C. Every house had experienced some loss  
D. The Buddha changed his mind  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** She could not find a house untouched by death, emphasizing the universality of grief and loss.

## Question 3
Which of the following best describes the meaning of the phrase “afflicted with” as used in the text?

A. Overjoyed by  
B. Cured of  
C. Affected by suffering or pain  
D. Delighted with  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** “Afflicted with” means to be affected by suffering, disease, or pain, as clarified in the text’s glossary.

## Question 4
According to the Buddha’s sermon, what is the consequence of continuous weeping or lamentation over the dead?

A. It brings the dead back  
B. It brings greater peace  
C. It increases one’s pain and suffering  
D. It is necessary for enlightenment  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The Buddha teaches that grieving excessively only increases personal pain and does not help the deceased.

## Question 5
How does the Buddha use the metaphor of “ripe fruits” in his sermon?

A. To illustrate the sweetness of life  
B. To show that only the old are in danger of death  
C. To demonstrate that all mortals are in constant danger of death  
D. To encourage people to eat healthy  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The metaphor explains that just as ripe fruits can fall at any time, everyone is always at risk of death.

## Question 6
Which of the following best captures the central difference between a sermon and a lecture, as discussed in the 'Before You Read' section?

A. A sermon is always humorous, a lecture is serious  
B. A sermon focuses on moral or spiritual guidance, while a lecture informs or teaches  
C. A sermon is only for children, a lecture is for adults  
D. There is no difference  

**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** The text introduces a sermon as a religious or moral talk, contrasting it with a lecture, which is more informational.

## Question 7
In the modern texts on grief, which of the following is NOT mentioned as a common feeling after the loss of a loved one?

A. Sadness  
B. Disbelief  
C. Excitement  
D. Guilt  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Excitement is not listed; the text mentions sadness, disbelief, and guilt as common feelings of grief.

## Question 8
How does the story of Kisa Gotami illustrate the concept of 'selfishness in grief'?

A. By showing she asked for help from many people  
B. By highlighting her initial inability to recognize the universality of suffering  
C. By her refusal to accept the Buddha’s advice  
D. By her neglect of religious rituals  

**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Kisa Gotami’s focus on her own loss, without realizing everyone experiences grief, is described as ‘selfishness in grief.’

## Question 9
What advice is given in the modern texts about helping others who are experiencing grief?

A. Give them lectures on moving on  
B. Avoid talking to them about their loss  
C. Reach out, be patient, and show support  
D. Tell them not to cry  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The modern text suggests reaching out, being patient, and showing support as the best ways to help those in grief.

## Question 10
What is the main message in the lines from Tagore included in the lesson?

A. Death should be feared  
B. Grief is a sign of weakness  
C. We should remember the life lived with thankfulness, not just mourn the loss  
D. Only the dawn can bring happiness  

**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Tagore’s lines encourage gratitude for the person’s life rather than simply grieving their absence, offering a positive perspective on loss.
