### Russian Wedding Customs  
- Simple planning: rings, bride’s dress, cars, reception  
- Expenses now shared by bride’s and groom’s families (previously bride’s family paid)  
- Wedding lasts 2 days, sometimes up to a week  
- Wedding procession: several cars  
- Groom must **climb stairs** and **answer questions/riddles** posed by bride’s friends to reach bride  
- If groom fails, must pay cash to proceed  
- Newlyweds take a city tour after registration  
- Reception: couple sits with family and guests; starts with **toasts** wishing the couple well  
- Groom kisses bride after toasts  
- Bride gets ‘stolen’ by friends; groom pays a fee to get her back  
- Bride’s friends steal bride’s shoe; groom pays for shoe  
- Guests enjoy watching these playful quarrels

---

### Indian vs Russian Weddings (Customs Similar/Different)  
| Customs similar to Indian weddings | Customs different from Indian weddings |
|-----------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Both can last multiple days        | Bride ‘stolen’ tradition unique to Russia |
| Families share wedding expenses    | Groom answers riddles to reach bride (Russian) |
| Toasts to the couple               | Simpler wedding planning in Russia    |

### Anton Chekhov’s "The Proposal"  
- Written 1888–89; one-act farce  
- Theme: wealthy families arrange marriages for **economic gain**, not love  
- Characters:  
  - **Stepan Stepanovitch Chubukov** (landowner)  
  - **Natalya Stepanovna** (his 25-year-old daughter)  
  - **Ivan Vassilevitch Lomov** (neighbor, wealthy, quarrelsome)  
- Lomov visits Chubukov to **propose marriage to Natalya**  
- All three are quarrelsome; petty disputes threaten proposal  
- Economic logic forces proposal to proceed despite quarrels

---

### Key Scenes & Conflicts  
- Lomov arrives in **evening dress** to propose  
- Lomov suffers from health issues (palpitations, poor sleep), wants quiet married life  
- Lomov and Natalya quarrel over **ownership of Oxen Meadows** (land dispute)  
- Both stubbornly claim Meadows as theirs, despite documents and history  
- Quarrel escalates with loud shouting, insults, and accusations  
- Chubukov sides with his daughter, refuses to concede  
- The quarrels reveal **pride and property valued over relationship**

---

### Important Definitions & Concepts  
- **Farce:** comedic play with exaggerated characters and improbable situations  
- **Wedding toast:** speech wishing couple well, followed by collective drinking  
- **Bride ‘stealing’:** playful tradition where bride is hidden, groom pays to retrieve her  
- **Palpitations:** medical condition causing irregular heartbeat, used by Lomov to justify urgency of marriage  
- **Economic marriage:** union arranged for financial or property gain, not love

---

### Exam Hooks  
- Theme of play: **marriage for economic advantage, not love**  
- Lomov’s proposal is overshadowed by **petty quarrels over land**  
- Russian wedding customs: groom must **answer riddles** to reach bride; bride can be **‘stolen’** by friends  
- **Toasts** symbolize community support & celebration  
- Wedding expenses now **shared by both families** in Russia  
- Contrast Russian wedding **simplicity** with Indian wedding **complexity**  
- Characters’ quarrels reveal **social values of pride and property ownership** over affection  
- Lomov’s health issues symbolize **pressure to marry for stability**

---

### Quick Reference: Key Quotes  
- Lomov: “I’m already 35 — a critical age... I suffer from palpitations...”  
- Natalya: “Oxen Meadows are ours!”  
- Lomov: “Oxen Meadows are mine!”  
- Chubukov: “The Meadows are ours!”  
- Play’s climax: **repeated shouting over land ownership, despite marriage proposal**
